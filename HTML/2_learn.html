Evidence & Impact by Violence Form

Verified statistical snapshots help us connect everyday experiences with national and global trends, showing why survivor-centered services must respond to multiple harms at once.

Physical Violence

  • 17% of ever-married Filipinas aged 15‚Äì49 have experienced physical, sexual, or emotional partner violence, with bruises and fractures still the most treated injuries in barangay health stations.[1]
  • WHO data show 27% of ever-partnered women worldwide endure physical and/or sexual intimate partner violence, often beginning before age 25.[2]

Emotional / Psychological Violence

  • Psychological violence‚Äîthreats, humiliation, coercive control‚Äîis reported by 19% of Filipinas in unions.[1]
  • RA 9262 filings confirm psychological violence remains the top VAWC complaint nationwide, signalling urgent need for trauma-informed barangay desks.[6]

Sexual Violence

  • 7% of women aged 15‚Äì49 have experienced sexual violence, and 3% reported assaults within the previous 12 months.[1]
  • Most survivors identify intimate partners or authority figures as perpetrators, underscoring how power imbalances drive coercion.[1]

Economic Violence

  • Violence against women carries productivity losses valued at up to 3.7% of national GDP through foregone wages, medical costs, and absenteeism.[3]
  • Filipinas shoulder 4.2 hours of unpaid care daily‚Äîroughly double men‚Äôs time‚Äîlimiting the savings needed to exit abusive homes.[8]

Digital Violence

  • 67% of Filipina respondents in the ‚ÄúFree to Be Online?‚Äù study experienced gendered cyber-harassment such as body-shaming, threats, or unsolicited images.[4]

SOGIESC-Based Violence

  • CHR and UNDP documented forced outing, conversion attempts, and denial of services targeting LGBTQIA+ Filipinos in schools, workplaces, and healthcare settings.[5]
  • Without a national SOGIE Equality law, survivors often rely on informal mediation that rarely stops repeat abuse.[5]

Systems View

Evidence on Root Causes & Risk Amplifiers

Numbers and policy reviews clarify why GBV persists despite existing laws, pointing to the structural shifts communities must push for together.

Persistent Gender Norms & Silence

  • More than half of women who experienced partner violence never sought help, citing fear of stigma or belief that abuse is a private matter.[1]
  • UN Women warns crises intensify violence when harmful norms go unchallenged, as seen during pandemic lockdowns.[9]

Economic & Care Burdens

  • Productivity losses from violence can reach 3.7% of GDP, underscoring how economic abuse undermines national development.[3]
  • Women‚Äôs unpaid care load‚Äîdouble that of men‚Äîlimits access to income, credit, and mobility needed to leave violent relationships.[8]

Institutional & Legal Gaps

  • Barangay VAW desks remain constrained by staffing and privacy limitations, affecting survivor trust and follow-through.[6]
  • The absence of nationwide SOGIE-inclusive legislation leaves LGBTQIA+ survivors with limited legal remedies and protection orders.[5]

Technology Outpacing Safeguards

  • Girls encounter persistent gendered harassment online, blurring boundaries between digital and in-person abuse.[4]
  • Cyber tips on online sexual exploitation tripled during lockdowns, overwhelming investigators and survivor services.[7]
